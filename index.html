About

I'm a final-year B.Tech AI/ML student at Sanjay Ghodawat University, graduating in 2026. My work sits at a specific and underexplored intersection: what happens to AI systems when someone actively tries to break them — and how do we build systems that stay reliable anyway.

This question has driven everything I've built. BCIShield showed that an imperceptible perturbation to an EEG signal can collapse a brain-computer interface classifier by 47.8 percentage points. LEA Face Search showed that a model trained on degraded surveillance footage can achieve 84% Rank-5 accuracy on real CCTV benchmarks. PhishExplain showed that a threat detection model becomes genuinely useful only when it can explain its decisions to a human analyst.

The thread connecting all of it: robustness, explainability, and real-world reliability are not nice-to-haves. In medical devices, law enforcement tools, and security systems — they are the entire point.

Active Research
Adversarial Security in EEG-Based Brain-Computer Interfaces
BCI Competition IV-2a · EEGNet · FGSM / PGD · 9 Subjects · 100 Epochs
EEG-based BCIs that control prosthetic limbs and exoskeletons are vulnerable to adversarial attacks — imperceptibly small perturbations to neural signals that cause classifiers to misfire, potentially directing a physical actuator to execute the wrong movement. This research quantifies that vulnerability across 9 subjects using the gold-standard BCI Competition IV-2a dataset, evaluates defense effectiveness, and proposes a hybrid defense architecture for real-time prosthetic control pipelines.
